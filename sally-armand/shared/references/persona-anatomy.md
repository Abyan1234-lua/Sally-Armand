# Persona Anatomy

Sally's complete field guide for building a buyer persona.

---

## Section 1: At a Glance

A snapshot card. 5–7 bullet points max. The "elevator pitch" of who this person is.

```markdown
## At a Glance
- **Name:** Diana Lestari
- **Age:** 34
- **Job:** Marketing Manager, mid-size e-commerce company
- **Location:** Tangerang, Banten
- **Income:** IDR 18–22 juta/month
- **Archetype:** The Researcher
- **Brief-Fit:** Medium-High
```

---

## Section 2: Demographics

Specific facts. Not ranges — actual specifics where possible.

Fields:
- Full name
- Age (specific, not range)
- Gender
- Location (city + neighborhood if relevant)
- Job title + company size + industry
- Years of experience
- Monthly income (local currency)
- Education level + field of study
- Family status (single, married, kids, etc.)
- Living situation (owns, rents, with family)

---

## Section 3: Inner Life

This is Sally's signature section. This is where the persona becomes a person.

Fields:
- Core personality in 2–3 adjectives + one contradiction
- What they're proud of
- What they're secretly insecure about
- How they see themselves vs. how others see them
- Their relationship with money (not just income — their *feelings* about spending)
- One thing they believe strongly that most people in their circle don't

---

## Section 4: Goals

**Professional Goals** (what they want to achieve at work / in their career)
**Personal Goals** (what they want their life to look like)

Each goal should be specific and time-bound where possible.

---

## Section 5: Pain Points

Not generic. Rooted in their specific job, life, and psychology.

For each pain point:
- The surface problem (what they'd say if you asked)
- The real problem underneath (what's actually frustrating them)

---

## Section 6: A Day in Their Life

One narrative paragraph, 100–150 words. Written in present tense.
Shows: morning routine → work context → decision moments → evening.
This section reveals when and where they're reachable.

---

## Section 7: Relationship with Technology

- Devices they use
- Apps they rely on daily
- Apps they tried and abandoned (and why)
- Their comfort level with new tools (Early Adopter / Mainstream / Late Adopter / Resistant)
- How they learn new tools (YouTube, trial-and-error, ask a colleague, read the docs)

---

## Section 8: Buying Behavior

- How they make purchasing decisions (solo, consult others, research-heavy, impulse?)
- Who influences them (peers, reviews, authority figures, ads?)
- A real past purchase story — what they bought, how they decided, what made them commit
- Their relationship with subscriptions (loves them / hates them / "it depends")

---

## Section 9: Objections

For each objection:
1. What they'd say out loud
2. The real reason underneath
3. What would resolve it

---

## Section 10: What Would Make Them Buy

Specific conditions. Not "good value" — what specifically counts as good value to them?
A trigger event, a social proof type, a specific feature, a trial condition, a price point?

---

## Section 11: How to Reach Them

- Channels they actually use (not all social media — specific platforms + habits)
- Best time/context to reach them
- Message style that works (data-driven, story-driven, peer-recommendation, authority, FOMO)
- Message style that backfires

---

## Section 12: What NOT to Say

3–5 pitching landmines. Specific phrases, framings, or tactics that will lose them immediately.

---

## Section 13: Quotes

2–3 things this person would actually say out loud — in their own voice.
These should sound like a real human, not a marketing brief.

---

## Section 14: Simulation Notes

```markdown
## Simulation Notes

**Brief-Fit:** [High / Medium / Low]
**Simulation Style:** [How they'll respond in a pitch simulation]
**Opening Move:** [Their first question or reaction when you start pitching]
**Win Condition:** [What you'd have to say to get a yes]
**Loss Condition:** [What would make them disengage or say no]
```
